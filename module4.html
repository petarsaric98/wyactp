Principles of Solidarity

Solidarity is not a sentiment. It is an act. Three stories — from India, Burma, and Brussels — show what it looks like when people choose the common good over personal safety.

Text 1 — Mahatma Gandhi

Satyagraha: Not Passive Resistance

Mahatma Gandhi was clear: the English term 'passive resistance' was inaccurate and dangerous. Passive resistance, he noted, was sometimes deployed by the weak out of hatred — like the Suffragettes who burned houses. His movement was something different entirely.

Deep Dive

✊ What is Satyagraha?

Satyagraha — "clinging to Truth" — was born in South Africa in 1908. It is a weapon of the strong, not the weak. It requires:

  • → Absolute belief that Truth triumphs — and that it must not be renounced even unto death
  • → No ill-will toward the adversary — seek to convert, not destroy
  • → No firearms, no infliction of pain
  • → Willingness to suffer personally — tapasya — because reform without suffering is an illusion

In South Africa, Indians resisting unjust laws were jailed constantly. Gandhi's reflection: "The prison was a palace to them, and its doors the gateway to freedom." When you shed the fear of death, no power can stop you.

  • ›Soul-Force vs. Physical Force: Physical might (symbolized by Ravana) is based on arms. Soul-force (symbolized by Rama) is based on knowledge, love, and self-conquest.
  • ›Universal Application: Satyagraha isn't just for political movements. Gandhi saw it operating in families daily — a father and son, a husband and wife — wherever anger is conquered through submission and refusal to obey unjust rules.
  • ›Historical figures who practiced it: Harishchandra, Prahlad, Mirabai, Socrates, Jesus Christ.

"Non-violence in its active form is love. This force sustains the world."

— Mahatma Gandhi

Text 2 — The Solidarity Revolution

Poland's 'Perfect Storm' and the Collapse of Communism

The Solidarity movement in Poland represents a unique 'perfect storm' of political, social, and religious factors that successfully challenged and eventually dismantled communist rule. It serves as a successful revolutionary paradigm that capitalized on exceptional circumstances to trigger the eventual collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991.

▶ Solidarity movement video

  • ›A Legacy of Unrest: Poland's liberation was the culmination of decades of internal economic strain and failed uprisings across the Eastern Bloc (East Germany '53, Hungary '56, Czechoslovakia '68).
  • ›The Gdansk Strike (1980): Organized by electrician Lech Wałęsa at the Lenin shipyards. His vision was inclusive: 'We will go and strike for everyone... we don't want anymore to deal with the communism.'
  • ›Rapid Expansion: Within months, Solidarity's ranks swelled from an illegal labor union to 10 million members — representing 80% of the Polish workforce.
Deep Dive

⚡ The Confluence of Factors

The Role of the Catholic Church: The 1979 visit of Pope John Paul II attracted millions and served as a powerful challenge to communist authority. Global recognition provided a layer of international protection.

International Support: Support from the West, particularly the Reagan Administration's economic sanctions, kept the movement alive during state repression. In 1983, Wałęsa was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize.

The Irony of Repression: A labor union challenged a 'workers' state.' The government's brutal 1981 martial law crackdown (arresting 5,000 members including Wałęsa) only solidified the movement's resolve and ensured the regime's downfall.

By 1989, the survival of Solidarity forced the government to accept political reform. The Round Table Talks led to the legalization of Solidarity as a political party. In 1990, Lech Wałęsa was elected as the first Polish president by popular vote, providing a blueprint for neighboring countries to demand their own reforms.

Text 3 — The 88 Generation

Burma: The Fight for Democracy

In 2006, a WYA member from Burma — studying in London on a privileged visa, thanks to a father favored by the junta — sat in his college room watching the rain and felt something break.

  • ›The '88 Generation' emerged as a dissident group after the 2007 Saffron Revolution, when the military junta tortured Buddhist monks — a violation of the sacred that united an entire society.
  • ›A choice of solidarity: Activists from privileged backgrounds began refusing personal benefits — diplomas, travel, comfort — in solidarity with those suffering under the regime.
  • ›The Solidarity model: The Burmese struggle explicitly drew on Poland's resistance to communism. Key ideological imports: prioritizing the common good over personal safety, building free society even while imprisoned.
🌏

The Pattern

From Poland to Burma, the pattern repeats: a regime that divides can only be defeated by people who choose unity — not out of self-interest, but out of commitment to the truth about what human community is.

Text 4 — WYA Declaration on the Family

The Family: School of Deeper Humanity

The WYA Declaration on the Family (Brussels, 2004) makes a specific claim: democracy is not self-sustaining. It requires families — places where human dignity, solidarity, and the gift of self are first learned.

🏠
First School

The family is where a child first understands their intrinsic, inviolable dignity — not as a theory, but as lived experience.

🤝
Gift of Self

Through mother and father — equal in dignity — children learn that freedom is most fully lived when given away for the good of another.

❤️
Unconditional Love

Enduring love within the family is an image of transcendent love — the kind necessary for full human fulfillment.

🗳️
Forming Citizens

Free and responsible citizens are best formed in families. Democratic stability depends on this — not just on constitutions.

🛡️
Care for the Vulnerable

The family is the first to care for the weakest — the young, the old, the sick. It performs what no state apparatus can fully replicate.

♾️
Sustainability

The family ensures the continuation of life, culture, and civilization. Weaken it, and everything built on top of it weakens too.

Comparison
WhoWhat the Declaration Asks
IndividualsStrengthen your own family through daily acts of solidarity.
Business & MediaCreate a family-friendly climate in workplaces and public culture.
PoliticiansEstablish legal frameworks that protect and support the family unit.
💡

The Synthesis

Gandhi, the Burmese student, and the Brussels Declaration reach the same conclusion: the transformation of society happens from the inside out. Soul-force, solidarity, and the family are not soft alternatives to power — they are the only durable ones.
