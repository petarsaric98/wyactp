I. The Philosophy and Practice of Satyagraha

Definition and Origins

Satyagraha  (translated as "clinging to Truth") was established in South Africa in 1908 to describe a unique power for redressing grievances. It is distinct from "passive resistance," which Mahatma Gandhi identified as an inaccurate term often associated with the weak or with acts of violence (such as those in the English Suffragette Movement).

Soul-Force vs. Physical Force:   Satyagraha  is pure "soul-force." While physical might (symbolized by the character Ravana) is based on arms and destruction, soul-force (symbolized by Rama) is based on knowledge, love, and self-conquest.

The Active State of Non-Violence:  Non-violence in its "waking state" is love. This force sustains the world; without it, ill-will would have led to global destruction.

A Weapon of the Strong:  Unlike passive resistance,  Satyagraha  can only be practiced by those with intense firmness of mind and fearlessness in the face of death.

Core Principles of the Satyagrahi

A practitioner of  Satyagraha  adheres to a strict ethical framework:

Persistence in Truth:  The belief that Truth ever triumphs and should not be renounced even unto death.

Absence of Ill-Will:  The practitioner seeks the destruction of the injustice, not the antagonist. They use no firearms and inflict no pain.

Self-Reliance:  A  Satyagrahi  does not wait for others but "throws himself into the fray," trusting that his practice will serve as a precept for others.

Acceptance of Suffering (  Tapasya  ):  Reforms require suffering. If the "fruit" of a movement is delayed, it is attributed to insufficient  tapasya .

Universal and Historical Application

The document asserts that  Satyagraha  is "all-pervading" and "infectious," applicable to all people regardless of gender or status.

Domestic and Civil Utility:  It is used perpetually between fathers and sons or husbands and wives to conquer anger through submission and refusal to obey unjust rules.

Historical Figures:  Examples include Harishchandra, Prahlad, Mirabai, Socrates, and Jesus Christ. Gandhi notes that while Christ’s  tapasya  was boundless, Europe has largely "disapproved" of his pure way of life, requiring further sacrifices for realization to dawn.

Political Application in India:  The document argues that India is uniquely suited for  Satyagraha  due to its knowledge of  dharma . It proposes using this force to secure the release of political detainees like Annie Besant and to achieve  Swaraj  (self-rule).

The struggle for democracy in Burma (Myanmar) highlights the practical application of solidarity and the influence of international movements on local resistance.

### Concrete Example: Gandhi's "Satyagraha" vs. Passive Resistance

Mahatma Gandhi insisted his movement in South Africa was entirely distinct from what the West called "passive resistance." 

He noted that the burning of houses by Suffragettes in England or their fasting in prison was classified as "passive resistance," yet it was often a weapon born of weakness and hatred. In contrast, Gandhi coined the term **Satyagraha** (Truth-Force or Soul-Force). Satyagraha is the weapon of the strong. It requires intense spiritual activity and absolute definiteness of purpose. 

A true *Satyagrahi* does not inflict pain on the adversary or seek their destruction; they seek to convert them through love and voluntary self-suffering. With the conviction that Truth is not to be renounced even unto death, they shed the fear of death entirely. In the cause of Truth, "the prison was a palace to them and its doors the gateway to freedom."

The "88 Generation" emerged as a potent dissident group, gaining renewed strength following the 2007 Saffron Revolution.

Catalyst for Unity:  The junta’s torture of monks—a violation of the sacred—enraged society and consolidated the opposition.

Rejection of Privilege:  Activists within the movement, sometimes from privileged backgrounds, have chosen to reject personal benefits (such as academic diplomas) in solidarity with those suffering under the regime.

The Influence of Solidarity

The Burmese struggle identifies closely with the Polish movement against communism. Key ideological imports include:

Common Good:  Prioritizing the nation's welfare over personal comfort.

Unity Against Division:  Resisting a power that seeks to "divide and control."

Building Society:  The goal is not merely to "survive the regime" but to actively build a free society, even in the face of incarceration and death.

III. The Family: The Fundamental Unit of Society

The  WYA Declaration on the Family  (Brussels, 2004) defines the family as the essential pillar of civilization and democracy.

The "School of Deeper Humanity"

The family is the primary environment where individuals learn what it means to be a human person:

Human Dignity:  Children first understand their intrinsic and inviolable dignity within the family.

The Gift of Self:  Through the complementary roles of mother and father (equal in dignity), children learn that freedom is most fully lived through the "gift of self."

Unconditional Love:  The family provides an experience of enduring love, which serves as an image of transcendent love necessary for human fulfillment.

Social and Political Functions

The family sustains the broader social fabric through several key roles:

Forming Citizens:  It is the place where "free and responsible citizens are best formed," thereby securing democracy.

Care for the Vulnerable:  The family takes on the essential task of caring for the weakest members of society.

Sustainability:  It ensures the continuation of life, culture, and civilization.
