The Person and the Law

Law exists for the sake of persons. John Finnis, Mary Ann Glendon, Jacques Maritain, and Muhammad Zufralla Khan explore the foundations of human rights and the priority of the human person in the legal order.

Text 1 — John Finnis

The Priority of Persons

John Finnis argues for a return to the foundational Roman principle: "All law is made for the sake of human beings." He warns that modern jurisprudence has drifted into a "de-personalized" view that treats people as mere objects of policy.

🏛️

"Since all law is made for the sake of human beings, we should speak first of the status of persons."

— Justinian, Digest 1.5.2
⚖️

"Knowledge of law amounts to little if it overlooks the persons for whose sake law is made."

— Justinian, Institutes 1.2.12

The Point of Law: Three Perspectives

H.L.A. Hart
Survival as "Natural Necessity"

Law serves the "minimum purposes" of beings constituted as men are. Because we are vulnerable and altruism is limited, rules prohibiting violence are a rational necessity for survival.

R. Dworkin
Law as Integrity & Equality

Law's point is to constrain government force through individual rights. A community only has legitimacy if it shows "equal concern" for all its members.

H. Kelsen
The De-personalized "Norm Particle"

The most extreme positivist. He argued the person in law is merely a "personification of legal norms"—a bundle of duties and rights. Finnis warns: This severing of law from humanity leads to judicial flight from reality.

The History of Regression

Finnis identifies key moments in legal history where the "point of law" was abandoned, treating human beings as mere property or policy determinations.

Scott v. Sandford (1857)

The Supreme Court declared members of the "African race" were not citizens and could be "lawfully reduced to slavery" as property. It was a radical failure to recognize that law is for the sake of persons.

Santa Clara County (1886)

The Court granted "personhood" to corporations via a ukase (an abrupt decree) without argument or reason, yet denied it to others. A checkerboard of arbitrary rulings.

Byrn & Roe v. Wade (1972-73)

Courts treated the unborn as a "policy question" rather than a biological reality. They argued personhood is a legal fiction rather than a natural fact, reflecting a failure of moral responsibility.

Roman Slavery Contrast

Roman law admitted all men are born free by nature, yet maintained slavery by "sheer power." They flouted justice while admitting the truth of personhood.

Philosophical Foundation

"Individual substance of a rational nature"

Finnis draws on Boethius and Aquinas to define the person. Unlike a mask (metaphor), a person is a unified self who understands, chooses, and carries out actions.

The "Radical Capacity"

A day-old baby has—radically, albeit not yet in usable form—the capacity to choose. This is the root of the dignity we all have, tenable even in the face of human inequalities.

🧠
Unity of Being

"The same I who understands, chooses, and senses... is a reality I already truly understand."

"Justice consists in respecting and promoting unyieldingly the good of members of a group who all are persons."

— John Finnis

Text 2 — Muhammad Zufralla Khan

Islam and Human Rights

Muhammad Zufralla Khan discusses how the right to differ lies at the root of all progress. He emphasizes that while the Declaration of Human Rights is not a draft Bill, it stands as a shining milestone in man's struggle for freedom and justice.

🌍

Deepening Consciousness

Islam seeks to stimulate and deepen the consciousness of duties and obligations, so that each of us may help to safeguard freedom and equality for all in all spheres of life.

Text 3 — Jacques Maritain

The Paradox of Agreement

In his introduction to the 1948 UNESCO Philosophers' Report, Maritain highlights a stunning paradox: while philosophers from antagonistic traditions cannot agree on the "Why" of human rights (the speculative), they can reach a spontaneous agreement on the "What" (the practical).

"

Yes, we agree about the rights but on condition that no one asks us why.

This "Why" is where the argument begins. Maritain argues that while speculative ideologies are fundamentally divergent, common practical ideas can serve as a "last resort to intellectual agreement," allowing a great task to be undertaken.

1 Mental Evolution

The "Geology of the Mind"

Maritain uses the metaphor of a "highly complex geology of the mind" to describe how moral knowledge and feeling grow like a plant, independent of philosophical systems.

Systems of moral philosophy are simply products of the intellect reflecting on ethical concepts that precede them. What matters most is the "apprehension by experience which occurs apart from systems."

🌱
Spontaneous Reason

Pre-scientific and pre-philosophical acquisitions of the human conscience naturally converge towards practical rules of behavior.

🤝
Pragmatic Convergence

A "new and wider declaration" where classical individualism and Marxism integrate purely pragmatically for action, despite theoretic opposition.

Speculative Antagonism

The Two Opposing Schools

⚖️
"Natural Law" School

Rights are fundamental, inalienable, and antecedent to society. They meet a prime necessity of the law and are the source from which social life originates.

📜
"Historical" School

Rights are relative to historical development and variable and in flux. They are a product of society itself as it advances with the forward march of history.

The Keyboard of Rights

Maritain uses a profound musical metaphor: acknowledging a list of rights is like having a keyboard. The music played depends entirely on the "Scale of Values" used by the society.

👤
Liberal-Individualist

Human dignity lies in the power to appropriate individually the gifts of nature to do freely what pleases the self.

⚙️
Communist / Collectivist

Human dignity lies in placing gifts under collective control to deliver man from the treadmill of labor and control history.

🕊️
Personalist / Co-operative

Dignity lies in bringing nature into service for the joint attainment of immaterial good and free self-determination of the person.

Possession vs. Exercise of "Inalienable" Rights

Because rights are human, they are subject to modification. Maritain notes that even where rights are "inalienable," we must distinguish between possession and exercise, dictated by justice.

The Criminal's Life

By his crime, he deprives himself not of his right to existence, but rather of the possibility of demanding that right with justice.

The Right to Culture

Subject to the "physical capacity" of society. Justice may forbid demanding it hic et nunc if it dissolves the social body, yet it remains a legitimate goal.

A Word of Promise

"

A Declaration of Human Rights agreed by the nations would be a great thing in itself, a word of promise for the downcast and oppressed throughout all lands...

— Jacques Maritain

Text 4 — Mary Ann Glendon

Foundations of Human Rights: The Unfinished Business

Mary Ann Glendon defends the Universal Declaration against charges of "Western imperialism," arguing that the true danger to human rights comes not from its origins, but from special interests attempting to hijack its legacy.

The Commission's First Argument

The "Maze of Ideology"

As the Commission debated the relation between man and society, impatience grew. Two distinct approaches emerged on how to handle the philosophical foundations of human rights.

👩🏽‍⚖️
Hansa Mehta

India · Human Rights Activist

A pioneering crusader against British colonialism and an advocate for women’s equality, she became impatient with the philosophical wrangling:

"We are here to affirm faith in fundamental human rights. Whether the human person comes first or the society, I do not think we should discuss that problem now. We do not need to enter into this maze of ideology."

👨‍🏫
Charles Malik

Lebanon · Philosopher

Called out of his Beirut classroom into public service, he had not yet perfected his suave diplomatic style. He rebuked her as a professor of the old school would chide a student:

"Whatever you may say, Madam, must have ideological presuppositions, and no matter how much you may fight shy of them, they are there, and you either hide them or you are brave enough to bring them out in the open and see them and criticize them."

1. Is the Declaration "Western"?

Critics often dismiss human rights as "cultural imperialism" because many nations were under colonial rule in 1948 and key ideas originated in Europe. Glendon dismantles this claim systematically:

1
Non-Western Leadership

Developing nations put human rights on the U.N. agenda. Figures like Peng-chun Chang (China), Charles Malik (Lebanon), and Carlos Romulo (Philippines) were the most influential members, possessing the rare ability to translate concepts across cultures.

2
Global Comparative Study

The draft was not pulled from Western documents alone. It was based on a 408-page study of global constitutions, with heavy input from nations like Egypt, India, Pakistan, Brazil, and Mexico.

3
Unanimous Multicultural Review

The 58-member committee that reviewed every word included massive diversity (Islamic, Buddhist, Communist, Latin American, and African nations). It passed without a single dissenting vote.

4
Post-Colonial Endorsement

Far from being a colonial vestige, almost all newly independent nations immediately adopted its principles into their constitutions and ratified subsequent UN Covenants.

5
Origin Does Not Dictate Value

While the elaboration of these concepts as "rights" occurred in Europe, UNESCO found that the underlying norms—freedom, dignity, tolerance—are highly prized across many traditions. Just as Civil Law isn't purely "Roman" today, human rights do not "belong" to the West. The origin matters less than whether the idea is conducive to human flourishing.

🚨
The Deconstruction Derby

Glendon warns of the hyper-individualistic dialect that treats rights as absolute while ignoring responsibilities.

  • ✗ Severing freedom from solidarity
  • ✗ Special interests hijacking rights language
  • ✗ Treating the poor as sources of harm, not allies

2. The True Imperialism

The real danger today isn't that the Declaration was Western in 1948, but that modern affluent nations and special interest groups are imposing their own agendas disguised as universal norms.

By isolating favorite rights and ignoring inconvenient ones, the seamlessness of the original vision—which linked freedom inextricably with solidarity—has been shattered.

Part V — The Mechanics of International Law

📜
Sources of Law

International law is derived from treaties (explicit agreements) and customary international law (consistent state practice followed out of legal obligation).

🔍
TMBs

Treaty Monitoring Bodies are independent experts who ensure states live up to their commitments. They issue authoritative recommendations to guide compliance.

⚖️
Opinio Juris

Opinio juris sive necessitatis is the belief that a practice is legally obligatory. It distinguishes a legal rule from mere habit or courtesy.
